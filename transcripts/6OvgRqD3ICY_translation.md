**0:00** · Gold, an element that has fascinated humans for millennia.

**0:05** · For the pre-Hispanic peoples, it had a ceremonial use.

**0:09** · It was a way to connect the earthly with the divine.

**0:14** · But when the Europeans conquered America they brought another vision.

**0:19** · For them it had a purely economic value.

**0:22** · So they began to exploit with ferocity zones rich in this precious metal.

**0:29** · And the Spanish crown became enormously enriched.

**0:33** · Today gold still has commercial value and is an integral part of the financial system.

**0:39** · Central banks keep it, along with the dollar, as a reserve to back their currency in the foreign exchange market.

**0:46** · And many see it as a safe haven asset that in times of instability maintains its value or can even increase it.

**0:54** · In Latin America and other parts of the world, however, it is also one of the vehicles for laundering money that comes from illicit activities, especially drug trafficking.

**1:06** · But why do cartels use gold? And what consequences does this have for the region?

**1:11** · Before talking about gold, we have to understand how money laundering works.

**1:16** · This concept began to be used in the early 20th century in the United States during Prohibition.

**1:22** · Since it was forbidden to sell alcohol, criminal groups that trafficked it needed to legalize the money they received.

**1:30** · And the famous mafia boss Al Capone found the perfect business: laundromats.

**1:38** · In addition to giving the name to "money laundering", in those "laundries" the three basic steps of asset laundering were established.

**1:46** · In simplified form, the process goes something like this: With illegal money a shell company is opened. Through it - or through straw men - the money is introduced into the financial system, this is known as Placement.

**2:03** · Criminals gradually deposit the illicit money into the business's bank account.

**2:09** · But to avoid paying taxes or having the crime discovered, they make constant transfers through transactions, investments, purchases of luxury items or other companies.

**2:20** · What they seek is to separate the money as much as possible from its origin.

**2:26** · This is called Layering.

**2:29** · Finally, the money, which is now "clean", is introduced back into the financial system, and in this way, the criminal benefits.

**2:38** · This is the phase called Integration.

**2:42** · With the "clean" money you can invest in legitimate businesses, personal purchases, or it can be used to create foundations or shell companies that will serve to launder money in the future.

**2:55** · However, the "laundered" money can leave some trace, such as receipts, accounting books, or the same accountants involved.

**3:03** · In fact, Al Capone was convicted of tax fraud after finding accounting records of his income on which he had not paid taxes.

**3:15** · But how is that practice transferred to gold and the drug business?

**3:19** · With drug trafficking, the bosses used a similar scheme to the three steps and much of the money they laundered was deposited in the United States.

**3:29** · Until, in the mid-80s, a law was passed that considered money laundering as a federal crime.

**3:36** · That forced drug traffickers to look for laundering methods that were not traceable.

**3:41** · And this is where gold comes in.

**3:43** · Because its chemical composition makes it very difficult to trace its origin.

**3:48** · Unlike diamonds, for example, which are easy to detect.

**3:53** · In addition, the gold market, especially in Latin America, is poorly regulated.

**3:59** · That allows it to be paid in cash and acquired from different sources.

**4:04** · This is how many drug bosses, especially in Mexico, realized that having gold items allows a certain anonymity.

**4:13** · In addition, gold is very malleable, so it is easy to convert it into another item, and make its tracking even more difficult.

**4:22** · The new object can be stored in a bank safety deposit box.

**4:26** · And over time, increase in value and generate profits.

**4:31** · For example, a bar can contain 400 ounces (11,000 grams) that can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.

**4:40** · But drug bosses have gone further. They have installed illegal mines in countries like Peru, Colombia, Venezuela and Brazil to obtain gold directly without intermediaries.

**4:54** · And here we see the classic money laundering scheme.

**4:58** · First they buy supplies and tools and set up illegal extraction mines.

**5:03** · These are located in areas with little state control and most transactions are done in cash.

**5:09** · Through a network of clandestine airstrips, this gold is transported to shell trading companies.

**5:17** · There they obtain a certificate that allows them to export it to legal markets.

**5:22** · Then, when the gold is already in regular markets, it is sold or used as a means of exchange and thus returns as clean money to the drug traffickers.

**5:32** · But this practice not only legalizes the wealth of the cartels, but also has a huge social impact.

**5:39** · Working conditions in illegal mines are dreadful.

**5:43** · There is child and sexual exploitation according to UN reports.

**5:48** · And the environmental cost is also very high.

**5:52** · To get one ounce of gold you need to remove about 8 tons of earth. That is, sacrifice a mountain to make a ring.

**6:03** · In fact, according to studies, illegal mining is the most widespread environmental crime in the Amazon and is one of the main causes of its deforestation.

**6:14** · Illegal mines also pose a health risk, because they use mercury and cyanide to separate the metal from the sediments.

**6:22** · And if these compounds reach the water, they can contaminate the fish that people eat and be lethal.

**6:30** · In southern Peru, in the region of Madre de Dios, is one of the main illegal gold mines in the world.

**6:38** · There, 180 tons of mercury are dumped per year, according to Peru's Ministry of Health.

**6:45** · Colombia, Brazil and Peru have begun to join forces to strengthen security at the borders. What they seek is to disrupt the flow of cash, which is the main source of financing for mining exploitation.

**7:00** · But experts and international organizations agree that efforts are still insufficient.

**7:07** · And until the creation of mining extraction points is stopped, money laundering through gold will be far from disappearing.
